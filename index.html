Chaos in a Driven Pendulum

This is a visualization of a 1987 chaos theory paper, concerning a certain high school-sounding physics setup. A pendulum is affected by three angular forces: friction, gravity, and a torque that varies sinusoidally in time (which is to say, first it goes one way, then the other). In the experiment from the paper, they use a motor to apply the third force, which for some reason is called the "kick".

These three forces all interacting with each other create an unsolvable system of equations, which makes the position of the pendulum completely unpredictable in the long term.

The graph is a plot of the state of the pendulum in phase space. A "graph in phase space" is just physics jargon for "the x axis isn't time". Here, the angle is on the horizontal axis and angular velocity on the vertical axis. The top means going fast counterclockwise, and the bottom means going fast clockwise. Stare at the animation for a bit, play around with the controls, and it should click.

By now you might be wondering what the deal with the swirly bit is. First, imagine tracing the pendulum's state on the graph over time. Better yet, just to see what that looks like. Interesting at the start, but eventually it'll just fill in the whole space.

But what happens if we record the state of the pendulum only when the kick is 0? Yep, it's the familiar swirly bit! Once you run the simulation for long enough (which isn't even very long), the state of the pendulum when the kick is a certain value will always correspond to a point inside the swirly bit for that particular kick.

Taking such a phase space sample at the characteristic frequency of the periodic system under consideration (whew!) is called a Poincare section, after Henri Poincare. It's one of the best-known ways to extract some predictions from a chaotic system, and certainly among the prettiest.
